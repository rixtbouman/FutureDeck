FUTURES DECK

A speculative oracle for exploring possible futures

How It Works Methodology The Cards Download

Futures Deck is a card-based foresight tool. Draw cards to create a "spread" representing a possible future, photograph it, and use AI to interpret the combination into a vivid scenario.

Designed for workshops, strategic planning, and speculative design sessions. The deck helps teams explore futures that might otherwise remain unimagined—surfacing assumptions, sparking conversations, and making abstract possibilities concrete.

How It Works

1

Draw Cards

Select cards from different categories to build a future scenario: an Arc (trajectory), Terrain (domain), Object (artifact), Wellbeing dimension, and Timeframe.

2

Lay the Spread

Arrange the cards on a table. Add a Prompt Card to guide the interpretation—"Describe this future" or "A day in the life."

3

Photograph

Take a photo of your spread. Each card includes machine-readable text that helps AI understand its meaning.

4

AI Interprets

Share the photo with ChatGPT, Claude, or another AI. It reads the cards and weaves them into a coherent future scenario.

Methodology

The Oracle Pattern

Futures Deck adapts the structure of tarot and oracle cards for strategic foresight. Like tarot, the cards don't predict—they provoke. Each draw creates a unique combination that forces consideration of futures you might not have imagined on your own.

Combinatorial Exploration

With cards across multiple categories, the deck generates thousands of possible combinations. A "Growth" arc in "Healthcare" with "Ritual" as key object and "Autonomy" as the wellbeing dimension creates a very different future than "Collapse" in "Education" with "Device" and "Security." The combinations surface non-obvious scenarios.

AI as Interpreter

Each card carries embedded context—not just a name, but semantic cues that help AI understand its role in the spread. When you photograph a spread, the AI reads these cues and synthesizes them into narrative. The AI doesn't replace human judgment; it accelerates the imaginative leap from abstract combination to concrete scenario.

Example Prompt Flow

You: [Photo of spread showing: Growth (Arc), Healthcare (Terrain), Ritual (Object), Autonomy (Wellbeing), 5 Years (Timeframe), + "Describe This Future" prompt card] AI: In this 2029 healthcare landscape, growth has transformed medicine into a deeply personal ritual. Daily health check-ins have become meditative practices—morning biosensor readings paired with breathing exercises, evening supplement routines that families perform together... [AI continues with specific details, characters, tensions, and implications]

Workshop Applications

Scenario Generation: Teams draw cards and generate multiple futures quickly, then compare and discuss which feel most plausible, most concerning, most desirable.

Assumption Surfacing: When a combination feels "impossible," explore why. What assumptions make it seem unlikely? Are those assumptions valid?

Strategy Stress-Testing: Draw a random future and ask: "How would our current strategy perform in this world?" Repeat with different draws.

What Makes a Good Scenario

The goal is a short story—vivid enough that you can picture living in it, plausible enough to think seriously about. A good scenario lets you evaluate: Could this happen? Do we want it?

Concrete details: Not "healthcare improves" but morning biosensor rituals, families doing supplement routines together, the specific texture of daily life. Abstract futures can't be evaluated. Concrete ones can.

Second-order implications: Good scenarios follow the chain. If autonomous vehicles succeed, what happens to parking lots? To teenage freedom? To drunk driving laws? To the rhythm of road trips? The interesting stuff is usually two or three steps out.

Internal consistency: The pieces should hang together. If the scenario has advanced AI, it shouldn't also have people doing manual data entry. Sloppy contradictions break the spell.

Avoid the generic: "People use technology to connect" could describe any decade. A useful scenario couldn't be copy-pasted into a different future.

Avoid the idealized: Utopias with no friction aren't believable. Good scenarios have tradeoffs, winners and losers, things that got better and things that got worse.

Mundane is fine: Sometimes the most useful scenario is today described at arm's length—making the familiar strange. Not every future needs flying cars.

Humor is fine: Funny scenarios can still be insightful. The absurd often reveals something true.

Specific technologies welcome: Want to explore what quantum computing means for encryption, or what brain-computer interfaces mean for privacy? The cards can guide you there. Specificity beats vague "tech advances."

Design Principles

Evocative, not prescriptive: Card imagery triggers associations without dictating specific interpretations. The same card means different things in different spreads.

Machine-readable: Each card includes text context so AI can interpret photos accurately. The text explains the card's semantic role without repeating the visible title.

Print-ready: Cards are designed with proper bleed margins for professional printing. Physical cards create shared artifacts that digital tools can't replicate.

The Cards

Arc

The trajectory—Growth, Collapse, Transformation, Discipline. Sets the overall direction.

Terrain

The domain—Healthcare, Education, Ocean, Commerce. Where this future plays out.

Object

The artifact—Ritual, Device, Vehicle. A tangible thing that matters in this future.

Wellbeing

Human needs—Autonomy, Impact, Security, Purpose. How this future affects people.

Timeframe

The horizon—Now, 5 Years, 10 Years. When this future arrives.

Modifier

Pace changers—Breakthrough, Setback. Accelerates or delays the timeline.
